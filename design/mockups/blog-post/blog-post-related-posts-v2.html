The Ultimate Guide to the Great Migration

By Barbara Buchel
October 2024 15 min read
Tanzania Kenya Safari Guide Wildlife

More than two million wildebeest, zebra, and Thomson's gazelle move in a continuous loop between Tanzania's Serengeti and Kenya's Masai Mara, driven by rain and grass. There is no fixed timetable. No guaranteed crossing date. The migration doesn't operate on a schedule that suits your travel agent — it operates on instinct, weather, and the ancient memory of where the grass grows sweetest.

Understanding the Great Migration means understanding the ecosystem. The herds follow the rains counterclockwise through the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem, roughly 1,800 kilometres annually. The spectacle you choose to see depends entirely on when you go — and each season offers something irreplaceable.

When is the Great Migration?

The migration is year-round, not seasonal. January and February bring the calving season to the Southern Serengeti — half a million wildebeest born in six weeks, drawing every predator in the ecosystem. March through May: the long rains. The herds scatter and the plains turn impossibly green. June through August: northward movement, and the first crossings of the Grumeti River. July through October: the famous Mara River crossings. November: the herds turn south again.

The River Crossings

The Mara River crossings are what most people picture when they imagine the Great Migration. A thousand wildebeest standing at the bank, milling, hesitating, then one animal commits and the rest pour in behind it. The water turns white. Crocodiles rise. The crossing lasts three minutes. Some make it. Some don't. It is the most dramatic thing I have ever witnessed in the wild, and I have been watching it happen for twenty years.

"The first crossing I watched lasted four minutes. I cried. The second time was worse."

On the Mara River, August 2023

The challenge is that crossings are unpredictable. The herds may stand at the bank for days before committing. You need time — ideally four or five nights in the Mara to have a reasonable chance of witnessing one. Camps positioned on the river, with vehicles on standby, give you the best odds. Your guide's network matters enormously.

How to Plan Your Trip

Most itineraries that work combine two or three locations timed to the calendar. Calving season means Southern Serengeti: Ndutu, Naabi Hill, the short-grass plains. River crossings mean the Masai Mara: Sekenani, Talek, or the Mara Triangle for the best concentration of crossings. If you have two weeks, you can follow the migration north from the Serengeti in July — arriving in the Mara just as the first crossings begin.

Private conservancies adjacent to the Mara offer what the national park cannot: night drives, walking safaris, off-road game drives, and near-empty sightings. For the crossings themselves, proximity to the river is everything. The camps that matter are the ones with vehicles positioned at the banks from first light. Your guide's radio network is your competitive advantage.
