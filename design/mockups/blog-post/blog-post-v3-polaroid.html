Tawana Camp Review: Natural Selection's Flagship Delivers — Almost

By Alfredo Seidemann
December 2025 12 min read
Botswana Okavango Delta Luxury Safari Lodge Review

Forty-five minutes on a dirt track from the airstrip. Dust on the dashboard, mopane scrub thinning as the Gomoti River pulls the landscape open. Then a woven boca tunnel — the kind of entrance that makes you slow down, lower your voice — and suddenly the Delta is in front of you. One of its arms, green and wide, stretching toward a horizon line that doesn't exist.

Tawana opened in May 2024. When I visited over Christmas 2025, it was barely 18 months old. And here's the context that matters: this is Natural Selection's first lodge they fully own and operate. Not managed. Not co-branded. Theirs. After years of building their reputation through North Island, Jack's Camp, and Tuludi — this is the property where they put their name on the door and said, "Judge us by this."

Eight suites on the Gomoti River, in the southeastern corner of Moremi Game Reserve. Their thesis statement as a Botswana safari lodge. So does it hold up? Mostly. Beautifully. With one caveat I'll get to.

The Vibe

The architecture hits you before the details do. Modern, but not trying to prove anything — think clean geometric lines softened by organic materials. Blues and turquoise and pale wood. Earthy tones pulled from the local Batawana tribal palette, though rendered in a way that feels contemporary rather than ethnographic.

The camp is unfenced. Completely. Elephants browse behind the suites. Baboons cross the walkways at 6 AM like commuters. An impala stood fifteen meters from the pool one afternoon, utterly indifferent. This isn't a lodge that promises wilderness proximity — it's a lodge where wilderness has voting rights.

"Impressive" and "unforgettable" aren't the same word.

On Tawana's search for its soul

Here's what keeps it from the top shelf. Walk through Duke's Camp — another Natural Selection property, older, rougher around the edges — and you feel personality radiating from every crooked bookshelf, every faded map on the wall. Jack's Camp has its own mythology. Xigera has its art collection. Tawana doesn't have that yet. It's gorgeous, immaculately designed, impressive in every measurable dimension. But beauty without eccentricity is just a very expensive hotel.

The Rooms

Call them suites and you'd be underselling it. These are villas. A hundred square metres each (130 for the family configurations). Living room, bedroom, bathroom — each zone distinct enough that you forget they're technically one structure. The spatial separation is deliberate, and it's one of the clearest improvements over North Island, where the open-plan approach sometimes felt like sleeping in a very luxurious fishbowl.

Cork flooring underfoot. Silent. Cool. A Jura coffee machine stocked with fresh ground beans, the bag marked with the origin farm. Private plunge pool on the terrace. An outdoor sala. One of those hanging basket chairs that looks ridiculous and is extraordinarily comfortable. Skipping a morning game drive and spending it on your terrace with that Jura espresso is not wasted time. It might be the point.

The Food (and the Wine That Saves It)

I'll be direct. The food at Tawana is fine. Not bad. Not exciting. Fine. This is a lodge that charges between $1,895 and $3,895 per person per night. At that price point, "fine" lands like a disappointment — not because it's objectively poor, but because everything else around it is operating at a higher altitude. The problem is the buffets. When your room has origin-labelled coffee beans and hand-selected South African gins, a buffet lunch feels like it belongs to a different property.

Now — the wine program. This is where Tawana overcorrects for the kitchen, and overcorrects beautifully. A dedicated walk-in wine cellar. An actual sommelier, trained in Stellenbosch. Standard included wines plus a premium selection that would hold up in most Cape Town restaurants. Wine tasting is offered as an activity between drives. I'd take a Stellenbosch Chenin Blanc in a bush cellar over another afternoon by the pool.

Wildlife & Activities

Moremi Game Reserve is a national park: sunset curfew at 6:30 PM, no walking safaris, no boating, no night drives. If you're comparing this to a private concession like Jao or Xigera, the activity menu looks thin. On paper, it is. In practice, the game concentration on the Gomoti River corridor makes the restrictions almost irrelevant.

What actually happened: multiple leopards across two days. A pair mating, close enough to the vehicle that I could hear them. Towers of giraffe backlit by the kind of light that only exists between a thunderstorm and sunset. Buffalo herds in the hundreds. And barely another vehicle in two days — Moremi's southeastern corner is, for now, under the radar.

"Botswana pricing justified. Rare sentence, that."

On whether the rates are worth it

The Verdict

Natural Selection built something extraordinary here. The most visually impressive lodge in the Okavango Delta. The design is sharp without being cold. The rooms improve meaningfully on everything they learned from North Island. The wildlife on the Gomoti River justifies the trip on its own terms, park restrictions and all.

What's missing? Personality. The 0.5 it didn't earn. Tawana has beauty, precision, and comfort — but not yet a story. It's 18 months old. These things can develop. Would I go back? Yes. For the leopards and the thunderstorm sunsets and the gin and that toilet with the view. Without hesitation.
