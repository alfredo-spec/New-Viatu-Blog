The Ultimate Guide to the Great Migration: Everything You Need to Know

By Barbara Buchel
October 2024 15 min read
What is the Great Migration? When Does it Take Place? Month-by-Month Breakdown Best Time to Go Where to Stay Tips for Planning FAQs

Ready to witness one of the wildest shows on Earth? The Great Migration is your ticket to nature’s most epic road trip. Over a million wildebeest, along with zebras and gazelles, journey across Tanzania and Kenya, making it one of the last massive wildlife migrations on the planet.

Here’s the kicker: while timing is everything, it’s unpredictable and depends on what you want to see. Even with all that unpredictability, getting front-row seats to the action is doable with a little planning.

We’ve been helping adventure-seekers plan safaris across Southern and East Africa since 2020, and our team (with over 10 years of safari knowledge) knows a thing or two about being in the right place at the right time. Below, you’ll find our ultimate guide with all our tips to help you plan the safari experience of a lifetime.

What is the Great Migration?

The Great Migration is the annual movement of millions of wildebeest, along with hundreds of thousands of zebras and gazelles, across the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem in East Africa. These animals travel in a continuous cycle through Tanzania’s Serengeti National Park and Kenya’s Masai Mara National Reserve, driven by the need to find fresh grazing land and water.

Spanning nearly 2,000 miles, the migration is considered one of the greatest wildlife spectacles on Earth. It involves dramatic river crossings, where the animals face dangerous predators like crocodiles and intense predator-prey interactions with lions, hyenas, and cheetahs along the way. This cycle of life and death is vital to the ecosystem, maintaining the balance between herbivores, predators, and the environment.

When Does the Migration Take Place?

A lot of people think the Great Migration only happens between July and October, during the famous river crossings, but that’s just one part of the story. The migration is actually a year-round event, constantly moving through different stages across the Serengeti and Masai Mara.

While the dramatic river crossings happen between June and October (also peak safari season), the rest of the year also offers incredible wildlife moments. From calving season in January to the massive herds spreading across the plains, there’s always something happening during the migration.

“Plan for more time rather than a quick in-and-out trip, as the herds move based on when and where the rain falls.”

Month-by-Month Breakdown

Here’s a general month-by-month breakdown to help guide your safari planning:

Calving Season
January
Southern Serengeti, near Lake Ndutu
Calving season begins — thousands of newborn wildebeest dot the landscape. Smaller groups scatter across the open plains.
February – March
Southern Serengeti
Calving peaks with 8,000 calves born daily. Predators — lions, cheetahs, hyenas — are in full force. Dramatic predator-prey scenes.
Breeding & Movement
April – May
Central & Western Serengeti
Long rains begin. Breeding season kicks off with battles between males. Massive columns of wildebeest stretch up to 40 km.
River Crossings
June
Western Serengeti, Grumeti River
Grumeti River crossings begin. Crocodiles lurk as herds break into smaller groups. Tension builds.
July – August
Northern Serengeti & Masai Mara
Peak Mara River crossings — legendary, heart-pounding action. Massive Nile crocodiles wait as wildebeest plunge into the water.
September – October
Northern Serengeti & Masai Mara
Herds split up, crossings slow. Consider private conservancies for fewer crowds and exclusive viewing.
Return South
November – December
Eastern & Southern Serengeti
Short rains arrive. Herds head back south, splitting into smaller groups. Fresh grass rejuvenates the plains — the cycle restarts.

When is the Best Time to Go?

The best time to catch the Great Migration depends on what part of the action you want to see:

  • Calving Season (Feb–Mar): Head to the southern Serengeti to witness thousands of baby wildebeest and intense predator action.
  • Rutting Season (Apr–May): Males battle for breeding rights in the Western and Central Serengeti.
  • Grumeti Crossings (May–Jun): The herds tackle their first river crossing in the central Serengeti.
  • Mara River Crossings (Jul–Aug): The legendary crossings in the northern Serengeti and Masai Mara. Timing is everything.
  • On the Move (Nov–Jan): Herds trek back south from the Masai Mara into the Serengeti.
Pro Tip
Even if you’re not aiming for the river crossings, accommodations near the southern Serengeti for the calving season (January to March) also fill up fast. Calving season is full of action with predators, so don’t miss out by booking late.

Where to Stay

Your choice of accommodation is a game-changer. Whether you’re chasing the Mara River crossings or soaking up the scenery during calving season, where you stay will influence how close you are to the action.

Permanent Lodges

If comfort and luxury are what you’re after, permanent lodges are your go-to. Think spacious rooms, private decks, plunge pools, gourmet meals, and top-tier service. They typically have more amenities — spas, infinity pools, even Wi-Fi.

  • Luxurious amenities (pools, fine dining, spas)
  • Perfect for those who want comfort after a long safari day
  • May require longer game drives to reach herds during different migration stages

Mobile Camps

Mobile camps follow the herds, setting up in remote, wildlife-rich areas. Canvas tents, bucket showers, and cosy campfires under the stars — though some can still be quite luxurious with comfortable bedding and en-suite bathrooms.

  • Right next to the migration, wherever the herds are
  • Immersive, authentic bush experience
  • Fewer amenities, but maximum wildlife encounters

Tips for Planning Your Safari

1. Book Early

The migration’s peak season (June to October) is a hot ticket. To secure the best lodges and camps, especially near the Mara River, you’ll need to book at least 12 months ahead.

2. Time It Right

Align your safari with the specific stage of the migration you want to experience. Your timing dictates your experience — calving season in the south (Jan–Mar), river crossings in the north (Jul–Oct), or the return south (Nov–Jan).

3. Beat the Crowds

Private conservancies adjacent to the Masai Mara offer the same wildlife viewing with fewer people, plus perks like night drives and walking safaris that aren’t allowed in the main reserves.

4. Mix Up Your Game Viewing

Consider ending your safari at a lodge away from the herds for tranquillity. This gives you the chance to see other iconic wildlife like the Big 5 without the hustle of the migration.

5. Add Side Trips

Extend your adventure: gorilla trekking in Rwanda, beach time in Zanzibar, Cape Town’s wine tours, or Victoria Falls for thrill-seekers.

6. Soar Over the Migration

A hot air balloon ride over the Serengeti or Masai Mara offers a sweeping, unobstructed view of the migration below. It’s a tranquil, intimate experience with no one else around.

Good to Know
The bulk of the migration happens in the Serengeti. River crossings are unpredictable — you might wait hours or days at a riverbank, or witness several crossings in one afternoon. For photographers, crossings are most dramatic around midday.
