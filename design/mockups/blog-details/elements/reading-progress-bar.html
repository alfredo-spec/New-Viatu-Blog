The Great Migration: A Complete Guide to Timing Your Safari

Alfredo Seidemann 12 min read March 2026

Ready to witness one of the wildest shows on Earth? The Great Migration is your ticket to nature's most epic road trip. Over a million wildebeest, along with zebras and gazelles, journey across Tanzania and Kenya, making it one of the last massive wildlife migrations on the planet.

Hero image: Wildebeest crossing the Mara River

Here's the kicker: while timing is everything, it's unpredictable and depends on what you want to see. Even with all that unpredictability, getting front-row seats to the action is doable with a little planning. The migration follows the rains, and the rains follow their own schedule. But patterns do emerge, and knowing them is your edge.

Understanding the Cycle

The Great Migration is not a single event but a year-round cycle. It's a continuous loop driven by the search for fresh grazing and water. The herds follow the rains across the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem, covering roughly 1,800 miles each year. It's the largest overland migration on the planet, and it never truly stops.

Think of it as a massive clock, with different "acts" playing out in different regions throughout the year. The calving season in the southern Serengeti. The long march northward through the Western Corridor. The dramatic river crossings in the north. And then the slow return south, completing the circle.

January to March: The Calving Season

The southern Serengeti plains, around the Ndutu area and the edge of the Ngorongoro Conservation Area, become a nursery. An estimated 500,000 calves are born in a span of just a few weeks, usually peaking in February. The short, mineral-rich grasses of these plains provide the nutrition the mothers need.

"Watching half a million wildebeest calves take their first wobbly steps across the Serengeti plain is one of nature's most quietly spectacular moments."

This is also prime time for predators. Lions, cheetahs, and hyenas know exactly when the easy meals arrive. The concentration of prey draws predators from across the ecosystem, making the calving season one of the best times to witness raw, unfiltered nature. It's not for the faint of heart, but it is unforgettable.

April to June: The Long March

As the southern plains dry out, the herds begin their long trek north and west. The columns of wildebeest can stretch for miles, a river of bodies flowing across the golden savannah. By May, the herds are typically in the central Serengeti, and by June they've reached the Western Corridor, where the first river crossings of the Grumeti River begin.

The Grumeti crossings are smaller and less publicized than those at the Mara River, but they can be just as dramatic. Enormous Nile crocodiles patrol the deeper pools, and the chaos of thousands of wildebeest funneling into the water creates scenes of extraordinary intensity. The camps in this area tend to be quieter and more exclusive, offering a more intimate experience.

Where to Stay

The Western Corridor offers some exceptional lodges and tented camps. Grumeti Serengeti Tented Camp and Singita Faru Faru are standout options. The area is less visited than the northern Serengeti, which means you might have a crossing all to yourself. That kind of exclusivity is increasingly rare in East Africa.

July to October: The River Crossings

This is the main event. The Mara River crossings are what most people picture when they think of the Great Migration. Thousands of wildebeest gather on the banks, building up courage for hours or even days before suddenly plunging into the churning, crocodile-infested waters. It's chaotic, dangerous, and utterly mesmerizing.

The herds cross and re-cross the Mara River multiple times during this period, moving between the northern Serengeti and Kenya's Masai Mara. Each crossing is different. Some are orderly, almost calm. Others dissolve into pandemonium. The timing depends on rain, grass, and whatever collective instinct drives a million animals to move as one.

Image: Wildebeest crossing the Mara River

The northern Serengeti, particularly the Kogatende and Lamai areas, offers the best vantage points for witnessing these crossings on the Tanzanian side. For those heading into Kenya, the Masai Mara's conservancies provide extraordinary access with fewer crowds than the main reserve.

Practical Timing Tips

Late July through September is generally considered the sweet spot. August is the busiest month for tourism, so consider July or late September for a less crowded experience. Keep in mind that no guide can guarantee a crossing on any given day. Patience is part of the experience. Build at least three to four nights in crossing territory to maximize your chances.

November to December: The Return South

The short rains arrive in East Africa around November, and the herds begin their long journey back toward the southern Serengeti. The Mara empties out, the camps become quieter, and the cycle begins again. This transitional period is often overlooked, but it offers excellent game viewing with far fewer tourists.

The landscape transforms rapidly. Within weeks of the first rains, the parched plains turn emerald green. The herds spread out as grazing becomes abundant, and the urgency of the dry season gives way to a more relaxed rhythm. It's a beautiful time to visit, and rates are often lower during this shoulder season.

Planning Your Trip

The single most important piece of advice: be flexible. The migration doesn't follow a calendar. It follows the rain. Your dates might need to shift by a few weeks depending on conditions. Work with a specialist who knows the ecosystem and has contacts on the ground who can advise on herd movements in real time.

Budget for at least seven to ten days if you want to experience multiple aspects of the migration. A combination of the southern and northern Serengeti, or the Serengeti and the Masai Mara, gives you the best chance of seeing a range of migration behavior. Flying between camps saves time and offers spectacular aerial views of the herds.

"The migration doesn't wait for you. You go where it leads, and that unpredictability is exactly what makes it so extraordinary."

Whatever window you choose, this is the kind of trip that stays with you. The sound of a million hooves. The dust rising in golden columns. The silence between crossings, when the only sound is the wind across the plain. It's Africa at its most elemental, and there's nothing else quite like it.
