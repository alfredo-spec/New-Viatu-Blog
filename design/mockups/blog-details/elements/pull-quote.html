The Great Migration is one of the most spectacular natural events on earth, involving over 1.5 million wildebeest, hundreds of thousands of zebra, and assorted gazelle making a continuous loop through the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem. It is not a single event but rather a perpetual journey driven by rainfall patterns and the search for fresh grazing.

"Plan for more time rather than a quick in-and-out trip, as the herds move based on when and where the rain falls."

On timing your Great Migration safari

Understanding this rhythm is essential for planning. The wildebeest calving season in the southern Serengeti between January and March is often overlooked in favour of the dramatic river crossings further north, but it offers its own extraordinary wildlife spectacle with predator activity at its peak.

Many first-time visitors assume the migration is a brief spectacle that happens in one location during a single month. In reality, the herds are always on the move, and the experience varies significantly depending on the time of year and the specific region of the Serengeti or Masai Mara you choose to visit.

"The migration is actually a year-round event, constantly moving through different stages across the Serengeti and Masai Mara."

Understanding the Great Migration cycle

From the calving grounds of the southern plains to the treacherous Mara River crossings in the north, each phase of the journey tells a different story. A well-planned itinerary takes this into account, positioning travellers at the right latitude for the right season.

The Mara River crossings are the most iconic moments of the Great Migration, where columns of wildebeest plunge into crocodile-infested waters in a desperate dash to reach the other side. These crossings are entirely governed by the animals themselves and cannot be predicted with any certainty.

"River crossings are unpredictable. You might wait hours or days at a riverbank, or witness several crossings in one afternoon."

The reality of migration safari planning

This unpredictability is part of the appeal. Experienced guides know the likely crossing points and can read the behaviour of the herds, but nature always has the final say. Patience and flexibility are the most valuable qualities a traveller can bring to this experience.
