What is the Great Migration?

The Great Migration is one of the most spectacular wildlife events on Earth. Each year, approximately two million wildebeest, accompanied by hundreds of thousands of zebra and gazelle, embark on a circular journey across the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem in search of fresh grazing and water. This relentless movement of animals is driven by the rains and the promise of nourishment, creating a cycle that has played out for millennia across the plains of East Africa.

The sheer scale of the migration defies easy comprehension. Columns of wildebeest can stretch for kilometres across the savanna, their hooves kicking up dust clouds visible from the air. The journey covers roughly 1,800 kilometres in a clockwise loop, crossing between Tanzania's Serengeti National Park and Kenya's Masai Mara National Reserve. Along the way, predators including lion, leopard, cheetah, and crocodile lie in wait, making the migration as dramatic as it is awe-inspiring.

Beyond the visual drama, the migration plays a crucial ecological role. The movement of millions of hooves churns the soil, distributing seeds and nutrients. The grazers trim the grasslands, stimulating regrowth and maintaining the health of the savanna ecosystem. Without the migration, the Serengeti-Mara landscape would look and function very differently.

When Does the Migration Take Place?

Unlike a single event with a fixed date, the Great Migration is a year-round phenomenon. The herds are always moving, always seeking fresh grass and water. However, the most dramatic and photogenic moments — the river crossings — tend to occur between July and October, when the herds move northward into Kenya's Masai Mara and must brave the crocodile-infested waters of the Mara and Grumeti Rivers.

The calving season, another extraordinary chapter, takes place from late January through March in the southern Serengeti. During this period, around 8,000 calves are born each day, creating an abundance of new life — and an irresistible draw for predators. This season offers some of the best predator-prey interactions anywhere in Africa, set against the backdrop of the Ngorongoro Highlands.

Because the migration follows the rains rather than a calendar, the timing can shift from year to year. A late or early rainy season will alter the herds' path and schedule, which is why experienced safari guides and operators monitor conditions closely to advise on the best windows for travel.

Month-by-Month Breakdown

In January and February, the herds congregate on the short-grass plains of the southern Serengeti and Ndutu area. The nutrient-rich volcanic soil here supports grasses that are essential for lactating females. Calving season is in full swing, and the sheer density of animals in this area makes for remarkable game viewing with minimal driving between sightings.

By March and April, the rains push the herds westward and northward. The columns begin to form, and the landscape transforms from golden brown to vivid green. May and June see the herds moving through the Western Corridor of the Serengeti, where the first major river crossings of the Grumeti River occur. These crossings are smaller in scale than the Mara crossings but equally intense.

From July through September, the herds are in the northern Serengeti and crossing into the Masai Mara. The iconic Mara River crossings — with their chaos, bravery, and heartbreak — take place during this window. By October, the herds begin their return south, and by November and December they are filtering back through the eastern Serengeti, completing the loop.

When is the Best Time to Go?

The best time depends on what you want to see. If dramatic river crossings are your priority, plan your safari for July through September in the northern Serengeti or Masai Mara. These months offer the highest probability of witnessing the chaotic, heart-pounding spectacle of wildebeest plunging into crocodile-filled rivers.

For an intimate and less crowded experience, consider the calving season in January and February. The southern Serengeti during this period is one of Africa's great wildlife theatres — newborn wildebeest taking their first wobbly steps, cheetah sprinting after young calves, and hyena clans patrolling the edges of the herds. The light is exceptional, and the wide-open plains provide unobstructed photography opportunities.

If you want to avoid peak-season crowds entirely, the shoulder months of May, June, and October can be excellent. The Western Corridor crossings in June are thrilling and see far fewer vehicles than the Mara crossings. Similarly, October's southward movement offers great game viewing with a sense of solitude that is increasingly rare in East Africa's popular parks.

Where to Stay

Accommodation in the Serengeti and Masai Mara ranges from luxury permanent lodges to seasonal tented camps that move with the herds. The mobile camps are a particularly compelling option — they position themselves along the migration route so that you are always close to the action, typically within a short game drive of the herds.

In the northern Serengeti, camps along the Mara River such as those in the Lamai Wedge and Kogatende area offer front-row seats to the river crossings. The Masai Mara's private conservancies provide excellent game viewing with fewer vehicles than the main reserve, along with the flexibility to do night drives and walking safaris.

For the calving season, the Ndutu area and southern Serengeti have a handful of well-positioned lodges and camps. Booking well in advance is essential for peak migration periods — the best camps often fill up 12 to 18 months ahead, particularly for July and August departures.

Tips for Planning Your Safari

Start planning early. The Great Migration is one of Africa's most sought-after experiences, and the best camps in the best locations fill up quickly. If you are targeting the Mara River crossings in July or August, begin your planning at least a year in advance. Working with a knowledgeable safari specialist who understands the migration patterns can make an enormous difference to your experience.

Be flexible with your dates. Because the migration follows the rains, it does not adhere to a strict schedule. A camp that was perfectly positioned last year might be slightly off the main path this year. An experienced operator will monitor conditions in real time and may suggest adjusting your itinerary even after booking to ensure you are in the right place at the right time.

Pack the right gear. A good pair of binoculars, a telephoto lens if you are a photographer, and layers for early morning game drives are essential. The Serengeti can be surprisingly cool at dawn, and dust is a constant companion on the plains. Neutral-coloured clothing — khaki, olive, beige — is standard safari attire and helps you blend into the environment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should I spend in the Serengeti for the migration? We recommend a minimum of four nights in the Serengeti to have a realistic chance of witnessing a river crossing. Five to seven nights gives you a comfortable buffer for weather and herd movement variability. Many travellers combine the Serengeti with other destinations such as the Ngorongoro Crater or Zanzibar.

Is the migration dangerous for visitors? Not at all. You observe the migration from the safety of a purpose-built safari vehicle, accompanied by an experienced guide. The camps and lodges are well-secured, and the wildlife is accustomed to vehicles. That said, you are in the African bush, so respecting the wildlife and following your guide's instructions is essential.

Can I see the migration with children? Absolutely. Many camps welcome families, and the migration is an unforgettable experience for children. However, some camps have minimum age requirements (typically six or eight years), and the long game drives can be tiring for very young children. A family-specialist safari operator can design an itinerary that balances game viewing with downtime and child-friendly activities.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I definitely see a river crossing? No crossing is guaranteed — this is nature. However, spending four or more nights in the right area during peak season gives you very high odds. Your guide will communicate with other vehicles and camps to track herd movements and position you for the best chance.

How fit do I need to be? Safari is not physically demanding. Most game viewing is done from vehicles, and camps handle all logistics. However, some walking safaris require moderate fitness, and the early starts can be tiring. Comfortable shoes and reasonable stamina are sufficient for the vast majority of safari experiences.

What about photography equipment? A 100-400mm or 200-600mm telephoto lens covers most scenarios. A wide-angle lens is useful for landscapes and camp shots. Bring ample memory cards, spare batteries, and a dust-proof bag. Many camps have charging facilities, though power schedules may be limited in more remote locations.
